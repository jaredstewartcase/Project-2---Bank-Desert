Financial Exclusion for the Unbanked and Underbanked


Introduction

  • Federal deregulation in the 1990s allows banks to pivot from primarily serving local communities to serving larger, more profitable geographic regions, leading to many small bank closures
  • The majority of these closures occurr in low-income communities and communities of color
  • Alternative financial services flood the market
    • Payday lenders, pawn shops, check cashing outlets
    • No credit checks or verification of income
    • Fees as high as 17% for a two-week loan; 300% annual percentage rates (APRs)
  • Fewer banks, more alternative financial services = marginalized communities risk incurring large amounts of debt and lose access to small business loans and mortgages

Thus, the communities most in need of financial inclusion are kept out of the system and remain unbanked (meaning, they have no checking or savings account) and underbanked (meaning, they have an account at an insured institution but also obtain financial services outside of the banking system).

In 2017, 20.5 million Americans were unbanked and 64.3 million were underbanked, with the majority being low-income people of color.
